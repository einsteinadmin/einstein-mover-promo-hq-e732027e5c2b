Horstman (Response A) Manager Tools
The scoreboard will crowd out feedback if you let it. A score documents feedback; it never delivers it. This design has that trap, and monthly re-qualification makes it worse. A BM who's underwater — new huddle cadence, hiring, Training Days, 81% turnover — will do the monthly review INSTEAD of feedback, not in addition to it. The system becomes the manager. "Check the matrix" replaces "when you left the dolly strap loose, here's what happened." That's role power substituting for relationship power, and relationship power is the only thing that retains movers you've already paid to season. No mover should ever be surprised by their status — that's the test. If a mover learns on the 1st that his clock reset to zero and hears it from a spreadsheet, not his manager, you've built a demotivation engine with good intentions. The one-month full reset is indefensible from a manager's chair: one bad month — a damage claim not fully his fault, a sick kid wrecking attendance — and he's back at zero? Managers will either stop delivering that news honestly or start gaming the inputs. Try three of the last four months at bar, or reset to month two, not zero. On the CEO's framing: fairness through automaticity is right, but it doesn't remove subjectivity — it RELOCATES it. Damages get contested, attendance gets coded, evals get signed. Whoever adjudicates those edge cases holds the real promotion power, invisibly; name that adjudication process now or fairness dies in the appeals. "Great metrics, bad attitude" is an adjusting-feedback problem: the CHOICES pass/fail only works if managers have documented, delivered redirects before the gate fires — wire it to the Coaching Protocol; no CHOICES fail without a feedback trail. And do not ship this as a manual monthly BM task — you'll get pencil-whipped reviews by month three. STRONGEST REC: every promotion-status change — up, down, or clock-reset — is delivered verbally by the manager in a scheduled one-on-one before it takes effect; the conversation, not the matrix, is the management.
Collins (Response B) Good to Great
Confront the brutal fact: "aligns with our attrition curve" should stop you cold. Your own flywheel names 81% turnover as the leak that's killing you; a ladder calibrated to the attrition curve isn't a development engine — it's a survivorship monument. It documents who happened to still be on the bus. Your stated strategy is seasoning acceleration, one year to four months; a ladder built on 480-hour and 1,924-hour gates is designed against your own hedgehog — it makes tenure the input when your flywheel says competence velocity is the input. Hours are a proxy for reps; measure the reps. If a mover demonstrates Master-level competence in six months, your flywheel wants him leading crews in six months — the hours minimum tells him to wait, and waiting movers quit. The fast-track (four perfect-10 months) is the tell: you already believe demonstrated performance can substitute for time. Now invert the architecture — make demonstrated, inspected competence the primary gate at EVERY rung, with hours as a floor for safety and credibility, not the pacing mechanism. That the training-app sprints gate only the first rung is backwards; your best development asset touches the ladder exactly once. On the CEO's pay-them-all stance: rigorous, not undisciplined — provided the bars are honest. First Who says keep the right people; artificial vacancy caps and forced rankings drive right people off the bus to satisfy a spreadsheet. A 9 is a 9. But the discipline lives in the standard: bars only mean something if managers actually fail people. The retroactive backtest is your bullet before the cannonball — fire more bullets: pilot the ladder in the Dallas beta branches alongside the training app, measure whether promoted movers season faster and stay longer, THEN roll company-wide. The monthly-review steady-bars element is the strongest part of the draft; keep it. The question for the authors: does this ladder cause seasoning, or record it? STRONGEST REC: rebuild every rung above the first around demonstrated competence gates (training sprints + inspected evals) with hours as a floor only — a ladder paced to your attrition curve institutionalizes the very leak your flywheel exists to fix.
Weaver (Response C) Compensation
This is more structure than most $24M companies ever build; the bones are right. You're right about pay grades above Lead: open-seat gating on pay levels is a vacancy lottery, not a comp system. Four-to-six levels per job with smaller increments SAVES money by keeping people on board while they grow more valuable — your ladder does that. Roles get gated by seats; pay grades get gated by value. You're also right to reject top-X% calibration, but for a different reason: forced distribution is rank-order thinking; you've built market pricing with performance gates. If everyone clears a 9, the question isn't "did we set the bar wrong" — it's "does the market price of a 9-performing lead support $21?" That's a data question you answer annually, not a quota. Where I'll challenge the CEO: "sustained performance" with a one-bad-month reset is a science rule with no art in it. A damage claim in month three of a 962-hour climb erases a year of excellence — the "don't let data blind you to common sense" trap. The fast-track as written — four consecutive perfect 10s once damages count — isn't an expedited path, it's a mirage; make it 4 months of Outstanding, not perfection. Run the backtest BEFORE you announce anything: a system that surprises payroll surprises trust. And the incentive administration is only half-built — you should have a system that monitors progress toward incentives and gives regular reports to employees. Keep CHOICES out of the number (correct), but require documented incidents, not vibes. STRONGEST REC: replace one-month-below-resets-everything with a 4-level rating scale where only "Developing" resets the clock — a pay system that punishes one imperfect month in an 81%-turnover industry teaches your best movers the ladder is rigged, and they'll climb someone else's.

Lencioni (Response F) Ideal Team Player
Keeping CHOICES out of the number is exactly right: the moment values become points, they become currency, and currency gets counterfeited. But challenge the framing: you're calling these rungs "pay grades." Some are. A lead runs a crew — that's a first LEADERSHIP seat, and it's where most companies quietly promote their Skillful Politicians. The "dream team but not player" mover isn't an edge case; he's the PREDICTABLE OUTPUT of a metrics-first ladder. Attendance, damages, driver score, contract efficiency — a hungry person with no humility can max every one while making three teammates want to quit. The metrics measure Hungry beautifully; they measure Humble and Smart not at all. So split the ladder: below crew leadership, automatic pay progression on the matrix is fine and fair; at the leadership threshold, human judgment must come back in — deliberately, not through a back door. On the pass/fail gate: pass/fail is the right shape, but "who judges" is the wrong question until you fix "with what evidence." A manager's gut is subjectivity; a manager's gut plus documented behavioral evidence is judgment. The instrument already exists — the coaching protocol. Two-actor sign-off is already proven in the training app — bring those on-site evals in; digitally captured, dual-signed evidence is the antidote to both manager favoritism and manager laziness. A failed gate must trigger a named-virtue conversation — "you're hungry and smart; here's where humble is missing" — with a coaching window. If a mover learns he was blocked and nobody told him why, you've taught your best people the system is political — the exact disease you built this to cure. STRONGEST REC: keep the matrix as the automatic pay engine below crew leadership, but make every promotion into a lead seat require documented, two-signature Humble-Hungry-Smart evidence plus a face-to-face virtues conversation — never a number alone.

⚠ Holds — with one condition

Call 1 — Streak engine (hours-weighted average)

Cameron's call: measure promotion streaks on an hours-weighted rolling average with a minimum floor — not calendar months, which punish part-timers and slow seasons.

Where it landed: all four voices accept the math as fairer — but it only ships if a mover can check his own status in the app in ~30 seconds (his score, the bar, his hours building). If that gauge can't be built, fall back to the simpler rule: "3 of the last 4 months at bar, none below the floor."

Unpack the detail

Hours-weighted means a 40-hour month can't count the same as a 160-hour month — the average runs over hours worked (roughly the last ~500), not over months. The floor is a minimum no single stretch can drop below, so one bad run can't hide inside a good average. The "30-second cab test" is the council's shipping condition: if a mover sitting in the truck can't understand where he stands in 30 seconds, the formula is too clever to publish — hence the app gauge requirement. Weaver added a refinement for consideration: check the floor across the full window, but let eligibility update on the most recent ~250 hours, so a mover who's genuinely improved gets rewarded quickly instead of dragging old months behind him.

Gauge = app dependency (Mike/Matisen) · numbers off the backtest

Cameron's call: keep the values (CHOICES) check as a simple pass/fail gate at promotion time — not a heavy evidence-collection process.

01

Streak rule — what pattern earns the promotion?

The question: "sustained performance" needs a formula. Option A — one bad month wipes the whole streak — is dead (Cameron, 7/2). Three candidates remain: B = 3 of the last 4 months at the bar, none below a floor (simplest, very recency-focused) · C = the streak pauses on a miss instead of resetting (gentlest) · D = a rolling average over your recent hours worked, with a floor (fairest math — Cameron's lean).

Where it stands: whichever wins must be hours-based (a 40-hour month can't count the same as a 160-hour month), recency-sensitive (old greatness can't hide current slippage), automated (the system pings the manager — nobody babysits spreadsheets), and simple enough that a mover reads his own status in 30 seconds in the app. The backtest picks the winning formula off real data.

Unpack the detail

D's average would run over roughly the last ~500 hours; a Weaver refinement lets eligibility update on the most recent ~250 hours so a genuinely improved mover is rewarded quickly. If C wins, it needs a completion window so a paused streak can't stay frozen forever. The council accepted the D engine but only behind the legibility gate — a self-serve gauge in the app (score, bar, hours building); if the gauge can't ship, the fallback is B (see Pass 2, Call 1).

Cameron, 7/3 (⚠ workshop): "A is easiest administratively but even that has hours discrepancies. ✅ Design resolved 7/2 late — pending workshop ratification

CHOICES gate — FINAL FORM ("fail-forward")

In plain terms: nobody is ever scored on the seven values, and nobody needs values paperwork to GET promoted. The gate exists in exactly one scenario — a promotion is about to happen and a manager says "not this person, not yet." That act is what requires the work: (1) a face-to-face conversation in values language ("you're hungry and smart — here's where humble is missing"), (2) a one-page form naming the virtue and the observed behavior, and (3) a 90-day path to clear it, with check-ins. Regionals read every single block. If no manager objects, CHOICES never touches the promotion at all.

Cameron's take (7/3): this is much closer to Anne's "lens, not gate" than the old wording made it sound. The block IS human judgment using CHOICES language — the form just guarantees the mover due process (told to his face, in writing, with a way out) instead of an informal, silent no.

❓ The real question for the room: when a manager's judgment says "no" to a promotion, does that no get due process — the conversation, the page, the 90-day path — or does it stay informal? That's the entire distance between Anne's lens and this gate.

Unpack the design history

This is the Weaver-confirmed final form and it supersedes two earlier designs: the "evidentiary" version (managers had to keep documented behavioral evidence to justify any block — died because depending on 10 branch managers keeping consistent ambient documentation is dead on arrival) and the "universal-logging composite" (log every redirect company-wide — died on Cameron's catch that logging rates have no denominator, so you can't audit what should have been logged). Oversight: regionals review 100% of blocks (volume is low), and per-branch praise-to-block asymmetry is a smoke-detector dashboard flag ONLY — odd ratios trigger a conversation, never an automated consequence. The integrity dashboard proper watches damage-assignment rates + attendance-coding per branch, baselined before launch.

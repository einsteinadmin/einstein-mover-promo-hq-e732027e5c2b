Cameron's Locked Positions

These are settled design calls, not workshop questions. Everything else on this page bends; these don't.

✓Purpose = fairness and pay equity first. The mediocre 2-year lead out-earning the excellent 6-month lead is the problem this ladder exists to fix.
✓Seats gate only up to Lead. Above Lead = automatic pay grades — NO vacancy caps. Roles get gated by seats; pay grades get gated by value. Nobody waits on an opening to be paid what they've earned.
✓NO top-X% bar calibration. Forced distribution is against the comp philosophy. If everyone honestly clears a 9, pay them all.
✓Backtest before publishing. No bar, rung, or rule goes to the field until it's been run retroactively against real mover data.
✓Fast-track yes — but calibrated to reality. An expedited path should exist; a bar nobody can clear is a poster, not a path.

Horstman (Response A) Manager Tools
The scoreboard will crowd out feedback if you let it. A score documents feedback; it never delivers it. This design has that trap, and monthly re-qualification makes it worse. A BM who's underwater — new huddle cadence, hiring, Training Days, 81% turnover — will do the monthly review INSTEAD of feedback, not in addition to it. The system becomes the manager. "Check the matrix" replaces "when you left the dolly strap loose, here's what happened." That's role power substituting for relationship power, and relationship power is the only thing that retains movers you've already paid to season. No mover should ever be surprised by their status — that's the test. If a mover learns on the 1st that his clock reset to zero and hears it from a spreadsheet, not his manager, you've built a demotivation engine with good intentions. The one-month full reset is indefensible from a manager's chair: one bad month — a damage claim not fully his fault, a sick kid wrecking attendance — and he's back at zero? Managers will either stop delivering that news honestly or start gaming the inputs. Try three of the last four months at bar, or reset to month two, not zero. On the CEO's framing: fairness through automaticity is right, but it doesn't remove subjectivity — it RELOCATES it. Damages get contested, attendance gets coded, evals get signed. Whoever adjudicates those edge cases holds the real promotion power, invisibly; name that adjudication process now or fairness dies in the appeals. "Great metrics, bad attitude" is an adjusting-feedback problem: the CHOICES pass/fail only works if managers have documented, delivered redirects before the gate fires — wire it to the Coaching Protocol; no CHOICES fail without a feedback trail. And do not ship this as a manual monthly BM task — you'll get pencil-whipped reviews by month three. STRONGEST REC: every promotion-status change — up, down, or clock-reset — is delivered verbally by the manager in a scheduled one-on-one before it takes effect; the conversation, not the matrix, is the management.
Collins (Response B) Good to Great
Confront the brutal fact: "aligns with our attrition curve" should stop you cold. Your own flywheel names 81% turnover as the leak that's killing you; a ladder calibrated to the attrition curve isn't a development engine — it's a survivorship monument. It documents who happened to still be on the bus. Your stated strategy is seasoning acceleration, one year to four months; a ladder built on 480-hour and 1,924-hour gates is designed against your own hedgehog — it makes tenure the input when your flywheel says competence velocity is the input. Hours are a proxy for reps; measure the reps. If a mover demonstrates Master-level competence in six months, your flywheel wants him leading crews in six months — the hours minimum tells him to wait, and waiting movers quit. The fast-track (four perfect-10 months) is the tell: you already believe demonstrated performance can substitute for time. Now invert the architecture — make demonstrated, inspected competence the primary gate at EVERY rung, with hours as a floor for safety and credibility, not the pacing mechanism. That the training-app sprints gate only the first rung is backwards; your best development asset touches the ladder exactly once. On the CEO's pay-them-all stance: rigorous, not undisciplined — provided the bars are honest. First Who says keep the right people; artificial vacancy caps and forced rankings drive right people off the bus to satisfy a spreadsheet. A 9 is a 9. But the discipline lives in the standard: bars only mean something if managers actually fail people. The retroactive backtest is your bullet before the cannonball — fire more bullets: pilot the ladder in the Dallas beta branches alongside the training app, measure whether promoted movers season faster and stay longer, THEN roll company-wide. The monthly-review steady-bars element is the strongest part of the draft; keep it. The question for the authors: does this ladder cause seasoning, or record it? STRONGEST REC: rebuild every rung above the first around demonstrated competence gates (training sprints + inspected evals) with hours as a floor only — a ladder paced to your attrition curve institutionalizes the very leak your flywheel exists to fix.
Weaver (Response C) Compensation
This is more structure than most $24M companies ever build; the bones are right. You're right about pay grades above Lead: open-seat gating on pay levels is a vacancy lottery, not a comp system. Four-to-six levels per job with smaller increments SAVES money by keeping people on board while they grow more valuable — your ladder does that. Roles get gated by seats; pay grades get gated by value. You're also right to reject top-X% calibration, but for a different reason: forced distribution is rank-order thinking; you've built market pricing with performance gates. If everyone clears a 9, the question isn't "did we set the bar wrong" — it's "does the market price of a 9-performing lead support $21?" That's a data question you answer annually, not a quota. Where I'll challenge the CEO: "sustained performance" with a one-bad-month reset is a science rule with no art in it. A damage claim in month three of a 962-hour climb erases a year of excellence — the "don't let data blind you to common sense" trap. Use a 4-level scale (Developing/Meets/Exceeds/Outstanding); let one Meets-month PAUSE the clock rather than reset it; reserve resets for Developing. The fast-track as written — four consecutive perfect 10s once damages count — isn't an expedited path, it's a mirage; make it 4 months of Outstanding, not perfection. Run the backtest BEFORE you announce anything: a system that surprises payroll surprises trust. And the incentive administration is only half-built — you should have a system that monitors progress toward incentives and gives regular reports to employees. Every mover should see their score, their bar, and their clock monthly in the app; that transparency is what makes the framework fund itself. Keep CHOICES out of the number (correct), but require documented incidents, not vibes. STRONGEST REC: replace one-month-below-resets-everything with a 4-level rating scale where only "Developing" resets the clock — a pay system that punishes one imperfect month in an 81%-turnover industry teaches your best movers the ladder is rigged, and they'll climb someone else's.
Einstein Advocate (Response D) House Voice
Where this turns the flywheel, concede plainly: a visible ladder with rising bars and monthly reviews is exactly "Build Field-Ready Crews Fast" made legible, and it ladders cleanly into the Q3 training theme and the training app. The separate CHOICES pass/fail gate honors the prior council lock — don't let one number drive exit, promotion, and pay. The CEO's no-vacancy-caps and rejection of top-X% calibration are pure house economics: if everyone honestly clears a 9, pay them all. Three doctrinal costs to name consciously: (1) The Senior Lead rung + "quarterly performance bonus" REOPENS A LOCKED COMP DECISION MID-YEAR. The May ladder was locked with hourly variable pay, $586K already added, 37.8% against a 40% ceiling, NI already dented. Comp decisions are precedent-setting — one mid-year reopen becomes the rule. If Senior Lead ships, it ships in the locked structure (hourly variable), not a new bonus mechanic. (2) One-month-below-resets-the-streak flirts with the ultimatum line: "pay corrections are not performance ultimatums" cuts both ways — don't let every performance dip become a pay event. A silent streak reset delivers a comp consequence where the Coaching Protocol demands a verbal redirect first; a score that resets pay progress without a human conversation routes around the development engine. (3) CHOICES pass/fail with no named judge risks making the values a checkbox — attitude currently lives nowhere in the weights; that's the real measurement gap. Backtest the bars before locking 7→9.5 — earned doctrine beats aspirational thresholds. STRONGEST REC: ship the ladder-with-rising-bars inside the LOCKED comp structure — no new bonus mechanic mid-year — and make any streak reset fire only after a documented verbal redirect, so the Coaching Protocol delivers the message before the scoreboard does.
Harnish-Ross (Response E) Scaling Up · Borda Winner
Good bones, three fights to pick. First, the fairness framing is right — fairness, not sameness; the mediocre 2-year lead out-earning the excellent 6-month lead is exactly tenure entitlement. And rejecting "only the top X% clear" is correct — that's ratcheting, and ratcheting is perceived as unfair and is detrimental to performance. If everyone honestly clears a 9, pay them all. But notice the load on the word "honestly": with the score built on two metrics weighted 50/50 and score integrity self-declared as the load-bearing wall, you've built a skyscraper on a two-legged stool. Run the backtest BEFORE you publish a single bar — what gets measured gets managed, and what gets MISmeasured gets gamed. Second, the one-bad-month reset is a carrot problem in a stick costume: pay is a status-laden, envy-inspiring, politically charged monster, and nothing feeds it like a mover who held a 9 for two months, slipped to 6.8, and restarts at zero. With 81% turnover your enemy is discouragement, not laxity — use rolling 3-of-4 or average-with-floor. Same logic kills the perfect-10 fast-track: a bar nobody clears isn't an expedited path, it's a poster. Third — the real landmine — resolve the quarterly-bonus vs hourly-variable conflict BEFORE rollout. Two overlapping variable schemes is how comp stays a topic instead of getting right and out of sight. Pick one lane: the promotion ladder moves BASE (competencies + sustained performance + market); variable stays the existing $2–$3/hr, period. And at 37.8% against a 40% ceiling with a $586K raise already denting NI, adding a Senior Lead rung the May ladder never priced is not a rounding error — the CFO needs to cost it first. Evals and reviews join the matrix eventually, but only individually-controllable, backtested inputs; "great metrics, bad attitude" belongs in the CHOICES gate and coaching. STRONGEST REC: publish no bar, rung, or reset rule until the retroactive backtest proves the score is gaming-proof and the CFO has priced Senior Lead plus the bonus-conflict resolution against the 40% ceiling — score integrity first, ladder second.
Lencioni (Response F) Ideal Team Player
Keeping CHOICES out of the number is exactly right: the moment values become points, they become currency, and currency gets counterfeited. But challenge the framing: you're calling these rungs "pay grades." Some are. A lead runs a crew — that's a first LEADERSHIP seat, and it's where most companies quietly promote their Skillful Politicians. The "dream team but not player" mover isn't an edge case; he's the PREDICTABLE OUTPUT of a metrics-first ladder. Attendance, damages, driver score, contract efficiency — a hungry person with no humility can max every one while making three teammates want to quit. The metrics measure Hungry beautifully; they measure Humble and Smart not at all. So split the ladder: below crew leadership, automatic pay progression on the matrix is fine and fair; at the leadership threshold, human judgment must come back in — deliberately, not through a back door. On the pass/fail gate: pass/fail is the right shape, but "who judges" is the wrong question until you fix "with what evidence." A manager's gut is subjectivity; a manager's gut plus documented behavioral evidence is judgment. The instrument already exists — the coaching protocol. Make the gate evidentiary: no open redirect pattern in the last 90 days, plus two or three written behavioral observations tied to specific CHOICES behaviors, reviewed by manager AND one peer-level second reader. Two-actor sign-off is already proven in the training app — bring those on-site evals in; digitally captured, dual-signed evidence is the antidote to both manager favoritism and manager laziness. One caution: at 81% turnover, don't let the values gate become a silent veto nobody explains. A failed gate must trigger a named-virtue conversation — "you're hungry and smart; here's where humble is missing" — with a coaching window. If a mover learns he was blocked and nobody told him why, you've taught your best people the system is political — the exact disease you built this to cure. STRONGEST REC: keep the matrix as the automatic pay engine below crew leadership, but make every promotion into a lead seat require documented, two-signature Humble-Hungry-Smart evidence plus a face-to-face virtues conversation — never a number alone.
